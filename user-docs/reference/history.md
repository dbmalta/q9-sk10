# Our Story

## Before 2004 — The Spreadsheet Era

The earliest version of what would become ScoutKeeper was not a system at all — it was a collection of spreadsheets and local databases used to manage a single scout troop.

## 2004 — Beginnings

It became clear that a web-based platform could serve the troop better, even with only around 60 members. Work began on a simple online system that allowed parents, scouts, and leaders to interact with the same platform. Web technologies in 2004 were limited compared to today, but the idea of a role-based, multi-level membership system was already taking shape.

## 2006 — Concept

The idea of expanding into a national scouting information portal was pitched — and shot down. Paper remained the primary way to disseminate and control information across the organisation.

## 2007 — Launch

ScoutKeeper was launched as an independent information portal on a personal initiative. The project was initially met with scepticism, with many assuming it would fizzle out within a few months.

## 2008 — Momentum

Within 12 months of launch, more than half the Scout Groups in the Association were using the system regularly. New features to assist the management of groups were steadily added.

## 2009 — Official Platform

The National Scout Council adopted ScoutKeeper as The Scout Association of Malta's official membership platform. The system served an organisation of around 3,000 members and handled membership records, training records, youth progress tracking, statistics, and a range of other day-to-day operational functions. For a period, ScoutKeeper also served as the booking engine for the national campsite — functionality that was eventually separated into a dedicated system, today known as [campsite.mt](https://campsite.mt).

Over the following years, the system went through several revisions with features being added continuously.

## 2014 — Incorporation

QuadNine Ltd was incorporated in 2014, with Lynx as a development partner. Ownership of ScoutKeeper was transferred to QuadNine, ending more than seven years of private funding for the system's hosting, maintenance, and upgrades. TSAM began paying a token licence fee to partly cover the costs of running the platform.

## 2020 — End of Life

By 2020, the system was built on technology approaching fifteen years old. Keeping pace with modern browsers and the expectations of members had become increasingly difficult. It was decided that ScoutKeeper had reached the end of its useful life, and TSAM would begin looking for a replacement.

## 2022 — Migration

TSAM adopted a new commercial membership platform and migrated its data from ScoutKeeper. The cost of the commercial system prompted some reflection on whether a capable, open-source alternative could fill that gap for organisations that cannot justify similar fees. At the time, it remained an idea rather than a plan.

## 2026 — ScoutKeeper 10

Over time, Kevin's involvement in the wider Scout movement gave him a clearer picture of the challenges facing smaller organisations. Many lack the resources or technical expertise to build or commission a proper membership system, leaving them to manage their affairs with spreadsheets and workarounds — the same starting point ScoutKeeper itself came from two decades earlier. Larger, better-funded associations have no such problem. The gap is real, and it shows.

Making ScoutKeeper 10 open source was not an obvious decision. It means forgoing potential revenue. But the alternative — a commercial product that prices out the organisations that need it most — felt like the wrong answer. An open-source platform that any organisation can copy, install, and use with minimal cost or technical knowledge creates a more level playing field.

The vision behind ScoutKeeper 10 has two sides. The first is practical: a Scout organisation exists to deliver scouting to young people, and it should not have to spend precious volunteer time and limited funds on administration that a well-built, freely available system could handle. Better tools mean better governance, and better governance means more energy left for the things that actually matter.

The second is less direct. The Scout movement has well-established policies and recommended practices for how organisations should be run — but translating those into day-to-day reality can be daunting, particularly for smaller groups without dedicated administrative support. ScoutKeeper 10 is designed with those processes built in, making it easier to simply do things the right way. For organisations that choose not to use the system at all, it can still serve as a working reference — a concrete example of how good governance practices can be implemented in practice, not just described in a policy document.

Development began in earnest at the start of 2026. ScoutKeeper 10 is a personal project by Kevin Camilleri, funded through QuadNine and developed with support from Lynx.
